Can you do a ROA breakdown for these companies?

To understand Return on Assets (ROA), we use the DuPont Decomposition. This method breaks ROA into two components to show whether a company's success comes from its profit margins or how efficiently it uses its assets.

The formula is: ROA=Net Profit Margin %Asset Turnover\text{ROA} = \text{Net Profit Margin \%} \times \text{Asset Turnover}ROA=Net Profit Margin %Asset Turnover

Here is the breakdown for Tractor Supply and Build-A-Bear based on their 2023 data:

1. Build-A-Bear (2023)

  • Net Profit Margin: 10.9% (For every $100 in sales, they keep $10.90 in profit)
  • Asset Turnover: 1.8 (They generate $1.80 in sales for every $1.00 of assets)
  • ROA Calculation: 10.9%1.8=19.6%10.9\% \times 1.8 = \mathbf{19.6\%}10.9%1.8=19.6%

2. Tractor Supply (2023)

  • Net Profit Margin: 7.6% (For every $100 in sales, they keep $7.60 in profit)
  • Asset Turnover: 1.6 (They generate $1.60 in sales for every $1.00 of assets)
  • ROA Calculation: 7.6%1.6=12.2%7.6\% \times 1.6 = \mathbf{12.2\%}7.6%1.6=12.2%

What does this tell us?

While both companies are performing well, Build-A-Bear has a significantly higher ROA (19.6% vs 12.2%). Let's look at why:

  1. Profitability Margin: Build-A-Bear has a much higher Gross Margin (54.4% vs 35.9%). Even though they spend more on SG&A (selling, general, and administrative expenses), they ultimately carry more of each dollar to the bottom line as Net Profit than Tractor Supply does.
  2. Asset Efficiency: Build-A-Bear is also slightly more efficient at "turning" its assets into revenue (1.8 vs 1.6). This suggests their store formats or inventory management might be leaner relative to the sales they produce compared to Tractor Supplys large-format rural stores.

Key Takeaway: Build-A-Bear is winning on both "levers" of ROAhigher profitability per sale AND higher efficiency in using their assets.

Note: All dollar amounts in the table are in thousands. For example, Tractor Supply's Net Revenue of $14,555,741 actually represents over $14.5 billion.

Why are these margins so low (or negative)?

It is important to remember that a low or negative margin doesn't always mean a company is "failing"though it often indicates a period of struggle. Here are a few common reasons:

  1. High Competition & Shipping Costs (Online): Companies like ASOS and Wayfair operate in the Online segment. They don't have stores, but they face massive costs for shipping, returns, and digital marketing, which can eat up all their profit.
  2. Turnaround or Restructuring: Macy's and Foot Locker have been undergoing significant business changes to adapt to new shopping habits. One-time costs related to closing stores or updating brands can temporarily pull margins down.
  3. High Volume, Low Margin Models: Some segments naturally have thin margins. For example, Walgreens and Dollar Tree rely on selling huge quantities of items at very low prices. They make their money through volume and efficiency rather than high profit on each individual item.

Educational Tip: When you see a negative Net Profit Margin, it means the company is reporting a Net Loss. If you look at the comparison tool for these companies, you will likely see that their Operating Profit Margin is also low or negative.

The Operating Profit Margin is calculated as:

Here are the three specific things this tells us about the two companies:

1. The "Safety Cushion"

Dillard's 14.5% margin means that for every $1.00 of sales, they have about 14.5 cents left over after paying for the clothes (COGS) and the store expenses (SGA). Macy's, on the other hand, only has 3.1 cents left.

  • The Lesson: If store costs (like electricity or wages) go up by just 4%, Dillard's is still very profitable. If they go up 4% for Macy's, their core business starts losing money. Macy's has almost no room for error.

2. The Impact of SGA (Operating Expenses)

If you look higher up the table at Gross Margin %, youll see they aren't actually that far apart (Dillard's 43.8% vs. Macy's 40.5%). They both mark up their clothes similarly. The real "gap" happens in SGA %:

  • Macy's SGA %: 34.6%
  • Dillard's SGA %: 26.1%
  • The Lesson: Macy's is spending $34.60 out of every $100 in sales just to run the stores and corporate offices. Dillard's is much "leaner," spending only $26.10. That 8.5% difference in efficiency is exactly why Dillard's operating margin is so much better.

3. Brand Power vs. Promotional Pricing

In retail, a low operating margin often means a company is having to use heavy discounts to move merchandise. If Macy's is constantly running "60% off" sales to get people in the door, their Operating Profit Margin will shrink because they are getting less revenue for the same amount of work.
