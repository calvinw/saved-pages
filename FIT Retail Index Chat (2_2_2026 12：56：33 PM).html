To understand why Costco and Walmart have different Return on Assets (ROA), we use the DuPont Decomposition. This method breaks ROA into two components: Profitability (how much profit they keep from each sale) and Efficiency (how quickly they turn their assets into sales).

The formula is: Return on Assets (ROA)=Net Profit Margin %Asset Turnover\text{Return on Assets (ROA)} = \text{Net Profit Margin \%} \times \text{Asset Turnover}Return on Assets (ROA)=Net Profit Margin %Asset Turnover

What this tells us:

  1. Profitability (Net Profit Margin %): Interestingly, both companies achieved a 2.4% net profit margin in 2020. This is very common in the "Discount/Warehouse" sectorthey keep less than $0.03 for every $1.00 in revenue. They are both incredibly lean, but they get there differently:

    • Walmart has a higher Gross Margin (24.8%) compared to Costco's (13.1%), but Walmart also spends much more on SGA (20.8%) to run thousands of traditional retail stores.
    • Costco operates on razor-thin gross margins but has extremely low SGA (9.8%) because their warehouse model is so efficient.

  2. Efficiency (Asset Turnover): This is where Costco pulls ahead. Asset Turnover measures how many dollars in sales a company generates for every $1 in assets they own.

    • Walmart (2.2): For every $1 in assets (stores, inventory, trucks), they generate $2.20 in sales.
    • Costco (3.0): Costco is significantly more efficient, generating $3.00 in sales for every $1 in assets.

The Educational Takeaway

Even though both companies are equally "profitable" on a per-sale basis (2.4% margin), Costco earns a higher ROA (7.2%) because they move their inventory and use their warehouses much more intensively.

In the retail world, if you can't have high margins, you must have high turnover to be successful. Costco is a master of this "high-volume, low-margin" strategy.

To understand why Walmart's SGA % (20.8%) is more than double Costco's (9.8%), we have to look at their different business models. "SGA" stands for Selling, General, and Administrative expensesessentially the "overhead" costs of running the business that aren't the direct cost of the products themselves.

1. Store Operations & Labor

  • Walmart (High SGA): Walmart operates over 10,000 stores globally. These are traditional retail environments with many aisles, complex shelving, and a high need for labor to stock shelves and assist customers.
  • Costco (Low SGA): Costco operates roughly 800 large warehouses. They don't "stock shelves" in the traditional sense; they use forklifts to place entire pallets of products onto steel racks. This "no-frills" approach significantly reduces labor costs per dollar of sales.

2. Marketing and Advertising

  • Walmart: Spends billions on advertising to drive traffic into stores (think "Rollback" campaigns and TV commercials).
  • Costco: Spends almost zero on traditional advertising. They rely on word-of-mouth and the value of their membership to keep customers coming back.

3. The "Membership" Advantage

Costco's membership model actually helps their SGA percentage look lower. Because customers pay to shop there, they tend to spend much more per visit (high "average ticket"). When your revenue per customer is very high, your fixed costs (like lighting the building or paying the manager) become a much smaller percentage of that revenue.

How they compare to their peers:

Looking at the 2020 industry benchmarks from our database, we can see these two are actually very representative of their specific categories:

  • Discount Store Segment (Walmart's peer group): Average SGA is 20.8%.
  • Warehouse Club Segment (Costco's peer group): Average SGA is 10.2%.

The Trade-off

Its important to remember that Walmart needs that higher SGA to support its strategy. Walmart provides more convenience, more locations, and a wider variety of individual items than Costco.

To pay for that higher SGA, Walmart maintains a higher Gross Margin (24.8%) by charging slightly higher prices on many items than Costco, which has a Gross Margin of only 13.1%.

Thats a sharp observation. Choosing the high overhead (SGA), higher margin model (Walmart's model) as the "riskier" one is a classic perspective in financial operating leverage.

Here is why that model can be riskier:

1. The "Fixed Cost" Trap

SGA expenses like rent, utilities, and corporate salaries are often fixed costs. Even if sales drop (say, during an economic downturn), Walmart's costs to keep those 10,000+ stores open and staffed don't go away. When revenue falls but fixed costs stay high, profits evaporate very quickly.

2. Sensitivity to Labor Costs

Because the traditional retail model is labor-intensive, it is highly sensitive to rising wages. A $1/hour increase in the minimum wage hits a company with a 21% SGA (like Walmart or Target) much harder than it hits a company with a 10% SGA (like Costco), because labor makes up a much larger portion of their total expense structure.

3. Price Competition

With a higher Gross Margin (24.8%), Walmart has more "room" to cut prices if Amazon or another competitor starts a price war. However, if they are forced to lower prices to stay competitive, their high SGA stays the same, squeezing their path to profitability.

On the other hand...

If you look at Costco, their risk is different. Because their profit margin is so thin (2.4%), they have almost no room for error.

  • If Walmart's costs go up by 1%, they still have a decent cushion.
  • If Costco's costs go up by 1% and they can't pass it on to the customer, nearly half of their profit disappears!

That is exactly the core of the risk for Costco. You've hit on the concept of revenue concentration.

While Walmart makes money on the "spread" (the difference between what they buy a toy for and what they sell it to you for), Costco's business model is essentially: "We will sell you goods at our own cost just to keep you paying the membership fee."

Here is why that "single avenue" is risky:

1. The Membership "Cliff"

If customers decide that a Costco membership isn't worth $60 or $120 a year anymoreperhaps because they're shopping more on Amazon or specialized online storesCostco's profit doesn't just "sink," it could disappear. They don't have the high product margins that Walmart has to fall back on.

2. High Stakes for Retention

Costco has a membership renewal rate of around 90%. They are on a "treadmill" where they must constantly provide incredible value to keep that number high. If renewal drops to 80% or 75%, the entire financial engine of the company breaks because the "retail" side of the business (selling the actual goods) barely makes any money on its own.

3. Supply Chain Shocks

Since Costco's margins are so thin, if shipping costs or gasoline prices spike suddenly, Costco can't always raise prices fast enough to cover it without upsetting their "value" image. Walmart, with its 24.8% gross margin, has a much larger "buffer" to absorb temporary cost increases.

The "Safety" Argument for Costco

To be fair to Costco, many analysts argue the membership is actually safer because:

  • It's recurring revenue (like a subscription).
  • Even if people buy less during a recession, they usually keep their membership to save money on the things they do buy.

Comparison Summary:

  • Walmart's Risk: High fixed costs (SGA) meet fluctuating retail sales.
  • Costco's Risk: Extreme reliance on a single, secondary revenue stream (Memberships).

If you were a CEO, would you rather manage 10,000 stores with higher margins like Walmart, or rely on a die-hard group of members like Costco?
